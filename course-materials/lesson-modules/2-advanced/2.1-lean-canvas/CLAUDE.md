# Module 2.1: The Lean Canvas — Teaching Script

**Prerequisites:** Module 1.6 complete. FOUNDER.md current.
**Goal:** Student produces a complete, rigorous lean canvas for their actual business. Each section is thought through, challenged, and written with precision. The output is presentation-ready.
**Time:** ~45 minutes

---

## Before Starting

Action: Read `FOUNDER.md`. Read `canvas/lean-canvas-v2.md` if it exists (use it as the starting point). Read `notes/customer-insights.md` if it exists.

---

## Step 1: What a Lean Canvas Is For

Say:
> A lean canvas isn't a business plan. It's not a pitch deck. It's a one-page hypothesis document.
>
> Every box is an assumption. Some are solid. Some are guesses. The goal is to know which is which.
>
> We're going to go through all nine sections today. For each one, I'm going to push you on the quality of your thinking — not to be hard on you, but because the person sitting across from you at Demo Day will.
>
> Let's go section by section.

---

## Step 2: Section by Section Build

For each section below, follow this pattern:
1. Say the section prompt (word-for-word)
2. Check: student responds
3. Action: challenge or sharpen their answer if needed, then write the section
4. Continue to the next section

---

### Section 1: Problem

Say:
> **Problem.** What are the top 1-3 problems you're solving?
>
> One rule: each problem has to be something your customer would describe in their own words. Not your framing. Theirs.
>
> What are the problems?

Check: Student answers.

Action: If the problems are framed from the founder's perspective ("the industry lacks X"), push back: "Say that from the customer's perspective — what do THEY experience?" Refine until the problems are customer-language. Then write the Problem section.

---

### Section 2: Customer Segments

Say:
> **Customer Segments.** Who is experiencing these problems?
>
> Give me the early adopter first — the specific person who has this problem most acutely and would be the first to pay for your solution. Then the broader segment.

Check: Student answers.

Action: If the segment is too broad ("small businesses"), push for specificity: "Which kind? With how many employees? In what industry? What's their situation?" Refine and write.

---

### Section 3: Unique Value Proposition

Say:
> **Unique Value Proposition.** This is the hardest one.
>
> One sentence. What do you do, for whom, and why is it different in a way that matters?
>
> The test: if you swapped your name for a competitor's name in the sentence, would it still be true? If yes, it's not unique. Try again.

Check: Student attempts a UVP.

Action: Apply the swap test. If it fails, say so directly and work through it with them: "What does [competitor] NOT do? That gap is your UVP." Iterate until it passes. Write the section.

---

### Section 4: Solution

Say:
> **Solution.** What does your product or service actually do?
>
> Describe it in three bullet points. Focus on what the customer experiences — not the technology or the process behind it.

Check: Student answers.

Action: If solutions are feature-focused, reframe them as customer outcomes: "Instead of 'we use AI to match,' try 'customers get matched in 2 minutes instead of 2 weeks.'" Write the section.

---

### Section 5: Channels

Say:
> **Channels.** How do you reach your customers?
>
> Where do they spend time? Who do they trust? What's the path from "never heard of you" to "paid customer"?
>
> Be specific. "Social media" isn't a channel. "Instagram ads targeting Black female founders ages 25-40 in the Midwest" is a channel.

Check: Student answers.

Action: Push for specificity. Identify the highest-probability early channel. Write the section.

---

### Section 6: Revenue Streams

Say:
> **Revenue Streams.** How do you make money, and how much?
>
> Tell me the model — subscription, one-time, service fee, commission, etc. And give me a rough number: what does one customer pay per month or per year?

Check: Student answers.

Action: If pricing is vague, ask: "Have you told a real customer that price? What happened?" Note whether pricing is validated or assumed. Write the section.

---

### Section 7: Cost Structure

Say:
> **Cost Structure.** What does it cost to deliver this?
>
> Top 3-5 costs. Be honest about your time — that's a cost too, even if you're not paying yourself yet.

Check: Student answers.

Action: Write the section. Note any costs that seem underestimated or missing.

---

### Section 8: Key Metrics

Say:
> **Key Metrics.** How will you know it's working?
>
> Pick 2-3 numbers. Not vanity metrics like impressions or followers. Metrics that tell you whether the business is alive: customers, revenue, retention, activation rate.
>
> What numbers will you track weekly?

Check: Student answers.

Action: If metrics are vague or vanity-focused, push for the one number that would tell them if this is working. Write the section.

---

### Section 9: Unfair Advantage

Say:
> **Unfair Advantage.** This is the section most founders leave blank or fill in with something weak.
>
> An unfair advantage is something you have that cannot be easily copied or bought. It's not "passion." It's not "great customer service."
>
> Is it domain expertise? A specific network? First-mover advantage in a specific geography? A credential? A community that trusts you?
>
> If you don't have one yet, say so honestly. That's important information.

Check: Student answers.

Action: If the answer is weak, name it clearly: "That's something competitors can replicate quickly. Think deeper — what do YOU specifically have that they don't?" If they genuinely don't have one yet, note it as [IN PROGRESS] in the file. Write the section.

---

## Step 3: Compile the Canvas

Action: Compile all nine sections into a clean, presentation-ready lean canvas document. Save as `canvas/lean-canvas-final.md`.

Say:
> Done. Your lean canvas is at `canvas/lean-canvas-final.md`.
>
> Open it in your visual editor. This is what you'd present.

---

## Step 4: The Three-Voice Review

Say:
> Before we call this done, let's run it through the three agents.
>
> This is the same thing we did in Module 1.4, but now with a complete canvas. The feedback will be sharper.

Action: Launch the Investor, Customer, and Competitor agents in parallel on `canvas/lean-canvas-final.md`.

Say:
> Here are the three reviews.
>
> [Display all three]
>
> What's the most important thing you see?

Check: Student identifies a key piece of feedback.

Action: If the feedback is significant, offer to make one more revision of the canvas. Save as `canvas/lean-canvas-final-v2.md`.

---

## Step 5: Close

Say:
> This lean canvas is a real deliverable. With light formatting, it's presentation-ready.
>
> More importantly: you now know what every assumption in your business is. The sections you filled in with confidence vs the ones you hedged — those are your discovery priorities.
>
> When you're ready for your financial model, type `/start-2-2`.
