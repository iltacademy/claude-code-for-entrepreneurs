# Module 2.3: The Pitch — Teaching Script

**Prerequisites:** Modules 2.1 and 2.2 complete. Lean canvas and proforma exist.
**Goal:** Student drafts a 5-minute founder pitch, refines it through agent review, and leaves with a document they can deliver at Founder Showcase or Demo Day.
**Time:** ~45 minutes

---

## Before Starting

Action: Read `FOUNDER.md`, `canvas/lean-canvas-final.md`, and `financials/proforma-90-day.md`. You have the full picture of this business. Use it.

---

## Step 1: What a 5-Minute Pitch Is

Say:
> Five minutes is not a lot of time. It's also not a little time if you know what to do with it.
>
> A 5-minute pitch has one job: make someone want to keep talking to you.
>
> Not convince them to invest. Not explain the whole business. Make them want to know more.
>
> The structure we use at ILT Academy is:
>
> 1. **The Hook** (30 seconds) — Why does this matter? Start with the problem, not the solution.
> 2. **The Problem** (60 seconds) — Make it real. Give it a face. Make the audience feel it.
> 3. **The Solution** (60 seconds) — What you do, for whom, and what changes.
> 4. **The Proof** (60 seconds) — What's real so far. Customers, revenue, conversations, traction.
> 5. **The Business** (30 seconds) — How you make money. The model, simply stated.
> 6. **The Ask** (30 seconds) — What you need next and why.
> 7. **The Vision** (30 seconds) — Where this goes if it works.
>
> That's it. Seven sections. Five minutes.
>
> Let's write yours.

---

## Step 2: The Hook

Say:
> Start with the hook. **What's the one sentence that makes someone lean forward?**
>
> A great hook is usually one of:
> - A surprising statistic
> - A short story about a real customer moment
> - A question that makes the audience realize they know this problem
>
> What's yours?

Check: Student attempts a hook.

Action: React honestly. If it's strong, say why. If it's weak, say: "That doesn't make me lean forward yet. What's the most surprising thing about this problem?" Work with them until it's sharp. Write the Hook section.

---

## Step 3: The Problem

Say:
> Now make the problem real. **Tell me about a specific person who has this problem.**
>
> Not "small business owners struggle with X." Tell me about one person. Their name, their situation, the moment they hit the problem.
>
> Who is it?

Check: Student describes a real or representative customer.

Action: Shape this into a 60-second problem narrative. It should: introduce the person, describe their situation, show the moment of friction, and make the audience feel the cost of the problem. Write the Problem section.

---

## Step 4: The Solution

Say:
> **What happens when your solution enters the story?**
>
> Continue from where we left off. Same person, same situation — but now you exist. What changes?
>
> Tell me in plain language, not pitch language.

Check: Student answers.

Action: Shape into a 60-second solution narrative. Keep it concrete and outcome-focused. End with the Unique Value Proposition from the lean canvas. Write the Solution section.

---

## Step 5: The Proof

Say:
> **What's real so far?**
>
> Any of these count: paying customers, letters of intent, pilot agreements, conversations with 10+ potential customers, revenue, signups, partnerships, awards.
>
> What do you have?

Check: Student answers.

Action: If they have real traction, lead with the strongest signal. If traction is light, frame what they have honestly without overselling: "We've had 15 discovery conversations, and 4 people asked how to sign up." Write the Proof section. Do not fabricate traction they don't have.

---

## Step 6: The Business

Say:
> **How do you make money?**
>
> From your proforma: [substitute the revenue model from their financials]. Give me one or two sentences on this, in plain language.

Check: Student states the model simply.

Action: Make sure it includes the price point and who pays. Write the Business section.

---

## Step 7: The Ask

Say:
> **What do you need right now?**
>
> This could be: investment (how much, what for), customers (who specifically, how to reach them), a partnership, a mentor, a grant.
>
> What's the ask?

Check: Student answers.

Action: Make the ask specific and time-bound. Not "we're looking for investment" — "we're raising $50K to hire our first part-time developer and close our first 10 customers in the next 90 days." Write the Ask section.

---

## Step 8: The Vision

Say:
> Last one. **If this works — if everything goes right — where does this go?**
>
> Not a moonshot fantasy. A grounded vision for what this looks like in 3-5 years. Who does it serve? What does it change?

Check: Student answers.

Action: Connect the vision to the mission they've expressed throughout — especially anything in FOUNDER.md about why they started. Write the Vision section.

---

## Step 9: Compile the Pitch

Action: Compile all seven sections into a clean pitch script at `pitch/5-min-pitch-v1.md`. Include:
- Section headers and time guides (e.g., "Hook — 30 sec")
- Full text for each section, written to be spoken aloud (not read)
- A word-count note at the bottom (target: 650-750 words for 5 minutes at a natural pace)

Say:
> Your pitch draft is at `pitch/5-min-pitch-v1.md`. Open it in your visual editor.
>
> Read it out loud if you can. Not in your head — out loud. It'll feel different.

Check: Student confirms they've read it.

---

## Step 10: The Agent Review

Say:
> Now let's run it through the three agents — but this time with a specific question for each.
>
> - **Investor**: What would make you want a second meeting?
> - **Customer**: Does this pitch make you feel seen as the person with this problem?
> - **Competitor**: What counter-narrative would someone pitch against this?

Action: Launch all three agents on `pitch/5-min-pitch-v1.md` with their specific questions.

Say:
> [Display all three reviews]
>
> One of these reviews will hit differently than the others. Which one?

Check: Student identifies the most useful feedback.

Action: Help the student make the most important revision. Save as `pitch/5-min-pitch-v2.md`.

---

>

>
> The pitch is yours now. It came from what you know, what you've learned, and what you believe is possible.
>
> That's the work. Go show it.
